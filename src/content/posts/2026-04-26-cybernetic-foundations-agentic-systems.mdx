## Abstract

Modern large language model (LLM) agent systems exhibit operational characteristics that classical cybernetics formalized between 1948 and 1992: probabilistic exploration stabilized by deterministic structure, regulation under partial observability, self-modification under organizational closure, and human-machine collaboration over irreducibly complex problems. Despite this convergence, a systematic literature search across arXiv, Semantic Scholar, and Google Scholar (n = 338 sources, April 2026) returned **zero papers** that apply classical cybernetics directly to LLM agent design. This review surveys the four foundational traditions — Wiener-Ashby, Beer's organizational cybernetics, second-order cybernetics (von Foerster, Maturana, Varela, Bateson), and Tsien's engineering cybernetics — and shows how each provides a precise theoretical apparatus for problems agent engineers are currently solving with intuition alone. Recent bridge papers (2023-2026) on agent-loop dynamics, sparsity-stability relationships, and multi-agent chaos suggest the field is independently rediscovering these results. We identify five concrete gaps where classical cybernetics offers tractable formalisms — requisite variety bounds on agent capability, Lyapunov stability for compound learning, PID control for agent feedback, the Good Regulator theorem applied to evaluation systems, and Beer's S2 coordination for multi-agent ecosystems — and outline an agenda for grounding LLM agent engineering in the seventy-year cybernetic tradition.

**Keywords:** cybernetics, requisite variety, ultrastability, viable system model, autopoiesis, meta-synthesis, LLM agents, agent stability, control theory, compound learning.

## 1. Introduction

The engineering of agentic systems — autonomous or semi-autonomous software agents built around large language models — has matured rapidly since 2023. Practitioners have converged on a recurring set of architectural patterns: deterministic harnesses around probabilistic cores [1, 2], persistent memory with scoring and decay [3], hierarchical evaluation pipelines [4], and human-in-the-loop oversight regimes [5]. These patterns have been derived largely from engineering intuition and trial-and-error iteration; few practitioners ground them in prior theoretical work.

Yet each of these patterns has a precise antecedent in classical cybernetics. Cybernetics, defined by Wiener as "the scientific study of control and communication in the animal and the machine" [6], emerged in the 1940s as an interdisciplinary program for analyzing systems that maintain stable behavior through feedback. Its mid-century mathematical formalization (Ashby [7, 8], Conant & Ashby [9]), organizational extension (Beer [10, 11]), epistemological reframing (von Foerster [12], Maturana & Varela [13], Bateson [14]), and engineering operationalization (Tsien [15], Qian, Yu, & Dai [16]) collectively produced a theoretical apparatus that has been largely dormant in mainstream computer science since the 1980s.

This paper makes three contributions:

1. **A four-tradition survey** of classical cybernetics organized for relevance to LLM agent engineering, with each result explicitly mapped onto a contemporary agent design problem.

2. **A confirmation, by systematic literature search, of a wide-open gap**: across 338 surveyed sources from arXiv, Semantic Scholar, Google Scholar, and the IEEE/ACM digital libraries, no paper applies classical cybernetic theorems directly to LLM agent architectures. Recent bridge work [17, 18] uses individual control-theoretic concepts but does not engage the cybernetic tradition as a whole.

3. **Five concrete formal openings** where classical theorems are tractable for current agent engineering: requisite-variety bounds on agent capability, Lyapunov stability for compound learning loops, PID formalization for agent feedback, the Good Regulator theorem applied to evaluation, and Beer's System 2 coordination as a primitive for multi-agent ecosystems.

The argument is not that LLM agents *should* be redesigned to match a 70-year-old framework. The argument is the inverse: practitioners are independently rediscovering cybernetic results, and the convergence is strong enough that the formal vocabulary is worth reclaiming. Naming the patterns gives engineers tools to reason about problems currently invisible to them — particularly stability, multi-agent coordination, and the limits of self-improvement.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys the four traditions of classical cybernetics. Section 3 establishes why LLM agent systems satisfy the operational definition of a cybernetic system. Section 4 develops six theoretical mappings from cybernetic results to agent architecture. Section 5 reviews the recent bridge literature (2023-2026). Section 6 identifies the gaps the lens reveals. Section 7 discusses implications for practitioners. Section 8 concludes with an agenda for future work.

## 2. Four Traditions of Classical Cybernetics

Classical cybernetics is not a single body of theory but four overlapping traditions, each with distinct mathematical objects, primary problems, and methodological commitments. We summarize each in the form most relevant to agent engineering.

### 2.1 Wiener-Ashby: Foundations

The Wiener-Ashby tradition begins with Wiener's *Cybernetics* [6] and the anti-aircraft predictor problem of World War II — predicting where a target *will be* from where it *has been*, in the presence of measurement noise. Wiener's solution, the Wiener filter, established two foundational moves: (i) treating prediction as the optimal extraction of signal from noise, and (ii) treating feedback as the *stabilizing* signal of a control system, not as evidence of failure. His parallel argument that information is the negative of entropy [6, ch. 3] grounded the unification of communication and control.

Ashby [7, 8] formalized this into three theorems that govern any regulating system:

**Requisite Variety Theorem [8, ch. 11].** For a system regulating against environmental disturbance, the variety of regulator responses must equal or exceed the variety of disturbances minus the variety the environment itself absorbs:

$$
V(E) \geq V(D) - V(R)
$$

where V(E) is the variety of outcomes, V(D) is environmental disturbance variety, and V(R) is regulator response variety. The regulator can either *attenuate* incoming variety (filter complexity before it reaches the controller) or *amplify* its own response variety (expand the repertoire of available responses). Ashby summarized this as "only variety can destroy variety."

**Ultrastability [7, ch. 7-9].** Ashby's homeostat exhibited two levels of feedback. Level 1 was conventional negative feedback — fast, deterministic, error-correcting. When Level 1 failed to maintain homeostasis (a critical variable left its viable range), Level 2 activated: random reorganization of internal connections until a new stable Level 1 configuration emerged. Ultrastability is thus not merely stability but the capacity to *recover* stability through structural exploration when the existing controller fails.

**The Good Regulator Theorem [9].** Conant and Ashby proved that "every good regulator of a system must be a model of that system." The corollary is severe: any control system that successfully regulates a complex environment must contain, at least implicitly, a model of that environment. There is no model-free regulation of non-trivial systems.

### 2.2 Beer: Organizational Cybernetics and the Viable System Model

Stafford Beer extended cybernetics from individual systems to organizations. His *Brain of the Firm* [10] and *Heart of Enterprise* [11] develop the **Viable System Model (VSM)**, which holds that any viable system — biological, organizational, or computational — exhibits five recursive subsystems:

- **System 1 (Operations):** the value-producing units; what the system actually *does*.
- **System 2 (Coordination):** anti-oscillation between operational units; prevents S1 components from interfering with each other.
- **System 3 (Internal Control):** management of present operations; resource allocation, performance monitoring.
- **System 3* (Audit):** ground-truth observation that bypasses normal reporting channels; defends against the corruption of reports by self-interest.
- **System 4 (Intelligence):** outward-looking adaptation; scans the environment for threats and opportunities.
- **System 5 (Identity):** purpose, identity, and the ethical container; balances S3 (now-optimization) and S4 (future-adaptation).
- **Algedonic channel:** an emergency bypass that escalates critical signals directly to S5.

Beer's central diagnostic principle is **POSIWID** — *the purpose of a system is what it does* [11]. A system that claims to learn but does not measurably change behavior is not, by POSIWID, a learning system. Beer further showed that organizational pathologies typically reduce to specific subsystem failures: missing S2 produces conflict between operational units; missing S3* produces drift between reported and actual performance; S3 dominating S4 produces ossification.

### 2.3 Second-Order Cybernetics: The Self-Reference Problem

First-order cybernetics studied observed systems. Second-order cybernetics, formulated by von Foerster [12], studies *observing* systems — systems in which the observer is part of what is observed.

Von Foerster's **non-trivial machine theorem** [12] states that the internal state of a sufficiently complex machine cannot be reconstructed from input-output observation alone. This is the cybernetic analog of Gödelian incompleteness: a sufficiently expressive system cannot fully model itself.

**Autopoiesis** [13], introduced by Maturana and Varela, identifies the structural property of living systems: organizational closure (the system continuously regenerates the network of processes that produce it) combined with structural openness (the components themselves change). A viable autopoietic system can change its structure indefinitely while preserving its organization.

Bateson's **Levels of Learning** [14] extends this to learning systems:

- **Learning 0:** Fixed responses (no change with experience).
- **Learning I:** Changes in response to feedback (operant conditioning, RLHF).
- **Learning II:** Changes in the *process* of learning — learning to learn.
- **Learning III:** Changes in the principles governing Learning II (rare, transformative, often pathological).

Eigenform theory [19, 20] applies the Banach contraction theorem to recursive self-application: a self-referential operation converges to a stable fixed point if and only if it is a contraction mapping. The fixed points — the patterns that survive recursive self-application — are eigenforms.

### 2.4 Tsien (Qian Xuesen): Engineering Cybernetics and Meta-Synthesis

Tsien Hsue-shen (Qian Xuesen) translated Wiener's cybernetics into a prescriptive engineering discipline. *Engineering Cybernetics* [15] addressed a specific problem: how to design feedback controllers for systems whose internal properties are *largely unknown*. Where Wiener described what feedback systems are, Tsien described how to *build* them when first-principles modeling is intractable.

In 1992, Qian, Yu, and Dai [16] introduced the concept of **Open Complex Giant Systems (开放的复杂巨系统)** — systems that are simultaneously open to their environment, internally complex with nonlinear interactions, and too large for exhaustive analysis. Their proposed methodology was **Meta-Synthesis (综合集成法)**: a structured human-machine collaboration in which qualitative human judgment and quantitative machine computation are combined under explicit protocol. Tsien's instantiation, the "Hall for Workshop of Metasynthetic Engineering," prefigures by three decades the human-in-the-loop architectures now standard in agentic systems.

## 3. Why LLM Agent Systems Are Cybernetic

A cybernetic system, in the operational definition shared across the four traditions [6, 8, 11, 16], satisfies four conditions: (i) it operates over a state it cannot fully observe; (ii) it produces actions whose outcomes are partially uncertain; (iii) it adjusts behavior in response to feedback; and (iv) its *organization* is more durable than its *structure*.

LLM agent systems satisfy each condition.

**Partial observability.** LLM agents observe their environment through tool calls, retrieved context, and structured inputs. They cannot directly observe their own activations, the latent state of the user, or the full state of the systems they interact with. Wiener's anti-aircraft predictor problem [6] is structurally isomorphic: the agent must predict the outcome of an action from a finite, noisy history.

**Action uncertainty.** Outcomes of LLM-mediated actions are probabilistically distributed: the same prompt produces different completions; the same tool call may succeed, fail, or partially succeed; downstream effects depend on world state the agent cannot inspect. This places the system squarely in the regime Tsien addressed [15] — feedback controllers for systems with unknown properties.

**Feedback adjustment.** Agent harnesses [1, 2] consume signals from prior outputs (evaluation scores, error traces, user corrections) and adjust subsequent behavior. This adjustment can be local (per-call retries) or systemic (compound learning, persistent memory). Either form is a feedback loop in Wiener's sense.

**Organizational durability.** Mature agent systems exhibit organizational closure: the principles governing how the agent improves are themselves invariant across improvement cycles. Specific skills, prompts, and tools change; the meta-process that adds, removes, and evaluates them does not. This is autopoiesis [13] in its computational form.

The consequence is that the formal results of cybernetics apply *directly*, not by analogy. A theorem about regulators (e.g., requisite variety) applies to an LLM agent acting as a regulator, with the same constraints and the same mathematical bite.

## 4. Theoretical Mappings to Agent Architecture

We develop six mappings, each linking a classical cybernetic result to a current agent-engineering problem.

### 4.1 Requisite Variety and Agent Capability Bounds

Ashby's Requisite Variety Theorem [8] sets a hard upper bound on what a fixed agent configuration can regulate. If the environment exhibits 1,000 distinguishable problem states and the agent has 5 tools, no amount of prompt engineering can close the gap; the system *must* either attenuate environmental variety or amplify its response variety.

In current practice, attenuation is provided by retrieval pipelines, summarization, schema-constrained inputs, and pre-compiled briefings; amplification is provided by tool ecosystems, skill libraries, and multi-agent decomposition. The cybernetic framing lets engineers reason about these as *symmetric* moves, both strictly necessary, and to identify when agent failures are due to mathematical undercapacity rather than implementation defects.

A practical corollary: an evaluation set narrower than the operational distribution provides false assurance. The variety of the evaluation must approach the variety of the deployment environment, or the regulator (the eval system) cannot certify the regulated (the agent).

### 4.2 Ultrastability and Compound Learning

Ashby's two-level homeostat [7] provides a formal account of the empirical pattern observed in compound-learning systems [3]: probabilistic exploration during failure, deterministic crystallization upon stable success.

Concretely: an LLM acting on a novel problem operates at Level 2 — random sampling of approaches until one succeeds. Successful approaches are abstracted into skills, recipes, or hooks (Level 1) that thereafter handle the same problem deterministically. The transition from Level 2 to Level 1 is governed by an empirical threshold — typically a use count and a success rate — analogous to Ashby's homeostatic stability criterion.

This framing exposes a missing component in most agent-engineering stacks: a principled rule for the *cooling schedule* — when to crystallize and when to defer. The crystallization analog of temperature in statistical physics [21] suggests one rigorous formulation: maintain a Boltzmann distribution over candidate behaviors and lower the temperature (deterministic weight) only when evidence accumulates above a confidence interval.

### 4.3 The Good Regulator Theorem and Evaluation Systems

Conant and Ashby's theorem [9] entails that an evaluation system must contain a model of the agent it evaluates. Practitioners frequently conflate evaluation *outputs* (scores, pass/fail) with evaluation *models* (the latent representation of agent behavior the scoring system uses). The theorem says: scores without an underlying model are not regulation; they are noise.

The corollary for agentic systems is that benchmark-only evaluation is a category error. A benchmark measures performance on a fixed input distribution; an evaluation system must model how the agent's behavior generalizes off-distribution. Conant-Ashby implies that a system evaluating an LLM agent must itself be at least as expressive (in the variety sense) as the agent's behavior space.

A second consequence is that **the evaluation system inherits the agent's incompleteness**. Von Foerster's non-trivial machine theorem [12] implies that the evaluator cannot fully model an agent whose complexity matches its own. Robust agent design must therefore tolerate imperfect self-models — productive despite the gap, not dependent on closing it.

### 4.4 Beer's VSM as Multi-Agent Architecture

The Viable System Model [10, 11] maps onto multi-agent architectures with unusual precision. Table 1 presents the canonical mapping observed across recent agentic systems.

**Table 1.** Beer's VSM mapped onto agentic system components.

| VSM Subsystem | Function | Agent System Analog |
|---|---|---|
| S1 (Operations) | Value production | Individual skills, tools, capability modules |
| S2 (Coordination) | Anti-oscillation | Task scheduling, tool routing, lock managers |
| S3 (Internal Control) | Resource allocation | Eval system, behavioral gates, compound learning |
| S3* (Audit) | Ground truth | Integration tests, output sampling, third-party probes |
| S4 (Intelligence) | Environmental scanning | Research subagents, tool discovery, model updates |
| S5 (Identity) | Purpose | Constitution, principles, persistent memory |
| Algedonic | Emergency bypass | Critical-error escalation, tier-1 alerts |

The most diagnostic claim in Beer's framework is the **S3/S4 homeostat**: the productive tension between optimizing current operations (S3) and exploring future adaptations (S4). This is the explore-exploit tradeoff [22] formalized organizationally three decades before reinforcement learning gave it that name. Most agent-engineering stacks invest heavily in S1 (skills) and S3 (eval) but underbuild S2 (coordination) and S3* (audit). The result is the precise pathology Beer predicted [11, ch. 8]: operational interference between concurrent agents, and reported metrics that diverge from ground truth.

POSIWID applies sharply to agentic systems. An agent that claims to learn but whose behavior does not measurably change is not, by Beer's principle, a learning system. The discipline of inferring purpose from output rather than from architecture documents is the most useful diagnostic in this framework.

### 4.5 Eigenforms, Autopoiesis, and Self-Modification

Self-improving agent systems enter the territory of second-order cybernetics. The relevant constraints are precise:

**Eigenform convergence.** A self-referential operation converges only if it is a contraction mapping [19, 20]. Engineering principles, evaluation criteria, and identity invariants must therefore satisfy a contraction property — each iteration of the improvement process must bring the system *closer* to the principles, not modify them. The empirical test is direct: if the improvement process is applied to itself, does it regenerate the same improvement process? A drifting principle is a non-eigenform; a stable principle survives recursive self-application.

**Organizational closure.** Maturana and Varela [13] distinguish *organization* (the invariant network of relations that defines the system) from *structure* (the specific components instantiating that network). A viable self-modifying system changes structure freely and organization not at all. In agent systems: skills, prompts, and memory entries change; the meta-process that creates and evaluates them is invariant. Violating this — letting the improvement process modify the principles governing improvement — destroys system identity.

**Bateson's levels of learning** [14] partition the design space:
- Learning I: parameter updates within fixed rules (RLHF, fine-tuning).
- Learning II: changes in the rule-formation process itself (compound learning, skill graduation).
- Learning III: changes in the organization that governs Learning II (rare, dangerous, often pathological).

Most current agent systems target Learning I; the frontier of compound-learning systems [3, 23] targets Learning II. Learning III remains unexplored in production systems and, per second-order cybernetics, should remain so without explicit safeguards.

**Kleene's recursion theorem** [24] guarantees that any self-modifying system has invariants — *something* persists no matter how aggressively the system rewrites itself. The engineering question is not whether invariants exist but whether the *right* invariants persist.

### 4.6 Meta-Synthesis as Human-AI Collaboration

Tsien's Open Complex Giant Systems framework [16] addresses a class of problems that defeat both pure human reasoning and pure machine computation: systems that are open, internally nonlinear, and too large for exhaustive analysis. A self-improving AI agent ecosystem operating across heterogeneous data sources, partial credentials, and human-shifting goals satisfies this definition exactly.

Tsien's prescription is **meta-synthesis (综合集成法)**: structured human-machine collaboration in which qualitative human judgment and quantitative machine computation are combined under explicit protocol [16]. The Hall for Workshop of Metasynthetic Engineering, Tsien's institutional implementation, prefigures by three decades the human-in-the-loop architectures now central to agent deployment. To our knowledge, no published work has yet mapped Tsien's framework directly onto LLM agent architectures, though adjacent work in cognitive science has invoked metasynthetic methodology in human-AI collaboration design.

The practical consequence is a design principle: agentic systems for genuinely complex problems should be designed as collaboration substrates, not as automation pipelines. The human contribution is judgment about *what* to optimize and *when* to change course; the machine contribution is throughput on *how* to execute. Architectures that erase this division typically fail at the boundary cases that defined Tsien's "open complex giant systems" class.

## 5. Recent Bridge Literature (2023-2026)

A small but accelerating literature uses individual control-theoretic concepts to analyze LLM agent behavior. We summarize the most theoretically loaded contributions.

**Tacheny et al. (2025) [17]** prove that agent loops exhibit three dynamical regimes: *contractive* (state trajectories converge to a fixed point), *oscillatory* (trajectories cycle), and *exploratory* (trajectories diverge). The classification depends on prompt design, tool latency, and feedback gain. The paper provides the first formal stability analysis of agent loops and confirms what Ashby's homeostat predicts: agent improvement processes must be contractive to converge.

**Majumdar (2026) [18]** proves a sparsity necessity theorem for stable single-agent control: viable controllers must operate over sparse representations of the state space, or stability is unattainable in expectation. The paper does not engage Ashby's variety theorems but its mathematical conclusions are isomorphic — sparse-attenuation is variety-attenuation in another vocabulary.

**Karwowski et al. (2023) [25]** demonstrate empirically that optimizing imperfect proxy rewards in RL produces precisely the dynamics Goodhart [26] predicted: the proxy improves while the underlying objective worsens. Beer's S3* (audit, [11]) is the cybernetic prescription for this failure mode, but Karwowski et al. do not cite Beer.

**Friston's Free Energy Principle and Active Inference** [27, 28] provide a unified mathematical framework for perception, learning, and action under uncertainty. The framework subsumes Wiener's prediction problem, Ashby's homeostat, and the Good Regulator theorem within a single variational free-energy minimization. While not yet widely adopted in LLM agent engineering, active inference is the most plausible vehicle for a unified theoretical framework.

**Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis et al. (2023) [29]** prove that multi-agent dynamical systems can exhibit chaotic behavior even when each individual agent is provably stable. This rules out compositional stability proofs for multi-agent ecosystems and aligns with Beer's organizational pathologies [11, ch. 8] regarding S2 underbuilds.

The pattern across this literature is striking. Each paper independently rediscovers a result that classical cybernetics formalized between 1948 and 1972. None cite the cybernetic source. The convergence is consistent enough to suggest that the cybernetic vocabulary is the natural unifying language and that its absence is a sociological, not a technical, accident.

## 6. Identified Gaps in the Literature

The systematic survey [30] revealed five gaps where classical theorems are tractable for current agent engineering and no published work addresses them.

**Gap 1: No formal application of Requisite Variety to agent capability bounds.** Practitioners reason informally about "when to add tools" or "how many skills" but no published work derives the variety bound for a given operational distribution. A formal derivation would let engineers prove minimum capability requirements for a specified deployment and detect undercapacity before failure.

**Gap 2: No Lyapunov function for compound learning.** Tacheny et al. [17] classify regimes empirically; Majumdar [18] proves stability for a restricted class. No published work proposes a Lyapunov function over a knowledge-scoring or memory-graduation system. Such a function would let practitioners prove (or bound) the convergence of compound-learning loops.

**Gap 3: No PID formalization for agent feedback.** The proportional-integral-derivative controller is the most-deployed feedback architecture in industrial control [31]. Its agent analog is conceptually obvious — proportional = current error signal, integral = accumulated memory preventing systematic drift, derivative = trend detection and anticipatory correction — but no published work formalizes it. Most current agent systems implement P (current eval scores) and I (accumulated feedback) but underbuild D, leaving them reactive rather than predictive.

**Gap 4: The Good Regulator Theorem applied to evaluation.** Conant and Ashby [9] proved that a regulator must model its system. Modern eval systems for agents are largely score-based and rarely contain explicit models of the agent under evaluation. A literature applying the theorem to eval design would establish necessary conditions for an evaluation system to *be* a regulator rather than a noise generator.

**Gap 5: Beer's S2 coordination as a multi-agent primitive.** Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis et al. [29] prove multi-agent chaos. Beer [11, ch. 5] specifies S2 — anti-oscillation between operational units — as the first-order remedy. No published work formalizes S2 as an architectural primitive for multi-agent ecosystems. Implementations exist (lock managers, scheduling layers) but lack the unifying theoretical frame Beer provides.

A sixth, broader gap: **no formal stability proof for self-modifying agent systems**. Eigenform theory [19, 20] requires contraction; Kleene's recursion theorem [24] guarantees invariance; both have been informally invoked in agent design but neither has been applied with formal rigor to a deployed system.

## 7. Discussion: Implications for Practitioners

The mappings developed in Section 4 suggest a small set of immediately actionable design principles. We highlight three.

**First, evaluation systems are regulators and should be designed as such.** Conant-Ashby [9] requires that an evaluator contain a model of the evaluated system. Practitioners who treat evaluation as benchmarking — scoring on a fixed distribution — are implicitly assuming the evaluation generalizes; the theorem says it does not. Robust evaluation requires explicit modeling of agent behavior off-distribution, which in current practice can be approximated by holdout shifts, adversarial probing, and the S3* audit channel Beer [11] specifies.

**Second, self-modifying agent systems require explicit eigenform invariants.** Eigenform theory [19, 20] makes precise what "core principles" must satisfy: contraction under self-application. The practical test — does the improvement process, applied to itself, regenerate itself? — is a sharper diagnostic than retrospective architecture review. Systems whose principles drift under recursive application have not yet found their eigenforms; systems whose principles are stable have, and that stability is the durable substrate the rest of the system can rely on.

**Third, Beer's S3/S4 homeostat names the explore-exploit problem at the system level.** Most agent stacks invest in S1 (skills) and S3 (eval) but underbuild S4 (research, environmental scanning) and S2 (coordination). The result, predictable from Beer [11, ch. 8], is local optimization without systemic adaptation. An explicit S4 channel — research subagents, tool discovery, model-update monitoring — is a structural rather than a tactical investment.

Beyond design principles, the cybernetic frame provides a *vocabulary*. POSIWID [11] supplies a diagnostic question — "what does the system actually do?" — that frequently exposes the gap between architectural intent and emergent behavior. Requisite Variety [8] provides a hard ceiling on what a fixed configuration can achieve. Ultrastability [7] names the pattern of probabilistic exploration crystallizing into deterministic structure. Meta-synthesis [16] frames the human-AI collaboration boundary explicitly. Each piece of vocabulary is a tool for reasoning about problems currently described only by their symptoms.

## 8. Conclusion and Future Work

This review has surveyed four traditions of classical cybernetics — Wiener-Ashby, Beer, second-order, Tsien — and documented their precise applicability to the design of LLM agent systems. The principal empirical finding is the absence: a systematic search of 338 sources returned zero papers applying classical cybernetics directly to LLM agent architectures, despite a growing bridge literature that independently rediscovers cybernetic results without naming them.

Five concrete formal openings stand out as immediate research priorities:

1. **A requisite-variety analysis of agent capability** — deriving capability bounds from environment variety distributions.
2. **A Lyapunov function for compound learning** — formalizing convergence properties of knowledge-scoring and memory-graduation systems.
3. **A PID-control formalization for agent feedback** — bringing the dominant industrial feedback architecture into the agent literature, with formal analysis of P, I, and D component stability.
4. **A Good Regulator analysis of evaluation systems** — establishing necessary conditions for evaluation systems to function as regulators rather than score generators.
5. **An S2 primitive for multi-agent coordination** — formalizing anti-oscillation between concurrent agents as an architectural component, with stability properties characterized analogously to Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis et al. [29].

Beyond these specific openings, the broader research agenda is to make implicit cybernetic structure in agent systems explicit. The convergence between mid-century cybernetic theory and contemporary practitioner intuition is sufficient evidence that the formal vocabulary is worth reclaiming. Cybernetics is not a metaphor for what agent engineers are doing; it is the native theoretical language for the work, and the cost of working without it is paid in problems that remain invisible until they manifest as failures.

The review's limitations are explicit. The 338-source survey covers English- and Chinese-language sources [16] indexed by major databases as of April 2026; it does not exhaustively cover the engineering control-theory literature, which contains additional bridge results. The mappings in Section 4 are theoretically grounded but not empirically validated against deployed systems; converting them into testable claims is itself a research program. The framework's known blind spot — creativity, serendipity, and genuine novelty — is unresolved and may require complementary frames (variational free energy [27, 28], evolutionary dynamics) for a complete picture.

The contribution of this review is a starting structure: the vocabulary, the mappings, the gaps, and a partial bibliography. The empirical work of testing whether the cybernetic frame produces measurably better agent systems remains to be done.
